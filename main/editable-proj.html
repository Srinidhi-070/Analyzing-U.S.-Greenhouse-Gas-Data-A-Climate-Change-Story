Atmospheric Carbon Dioxide Concentrations and Climate Change

This project focuses on analyzing atmospheric carbon dioxide (CO₂) data to illustrate the long-term trends and how they correlate with the increasing severity of global warming. By presenting the data in a narrative format, we aim to communicate the urgency of addressing climate change.

Understanding Atmospheric CO₂ Levels and Climate Change

Why CO₂ Matters

Carbon dioxide is Earth’s most important greenhouse gas: a gas that absorbs and radiates heat. Unlike oxygen or nitrogen (which make up most of our atmosphere), greenhouse gases absorb heat radiating from the Earth’s surface and re-release it in all directions—including back toward Earth’s surface. Without carbon dioxide, Earth’s natural greenhouse effect would be too weak to keep the average global surface temperature above freezing. By adding more carbon dioxide to the atmosphere, people are supercharging the natural greenhouse effect, causing global temperature to rise. According to observations by the NOAA Global Monitoring Lab, in 2021 carbon dioxide alone was responsible for about two-thirds of the total heating influence of all human-produced greenhouse gases. Another reason carbon dioxide is important in the Earth system is that it dissolves into the ocean like the fizz in a can of soda. It reacts with water molecules, producing carbonic acid and lowering the ocean's pH (raising its acidity). Since the start of the Industrial Revolution, the pH of the ocean's surface waters has dropped from 8.21 to 8.10. This drop in pH is called ocean acidification.

NOAA's Role in Climate Monitoring

The National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration (NOAA) plays a critical role in monitoring greenhouse gases. Their Global Greenhouse Gas Reference Network (GGGRN) tracks concentrations of CO₂, methane (CH₄), nitrous oxide (N₂O), and other trace gases. By collecting data from surface-level sites and high-altitude measurements, NOAA helps build a comprehensive understanding of the distribution and trends of greenhouse gases worldwide. How Rising CO₂ Levels Affect Our Planet

Rising CO₂ levels are linked to more frequent and severe climate events, including:

  • Global Temperature Rise: Increased CO₂ traps more heat in the Earth's atmosphere, leading to rising global temperatures.
  • Ocean Acidification: About 30% of emitted CO₂ is absorbed by oceans, leading to acidification that harms marine life.
  • Melting Ice Caps: Higher global temperatures contribute to the melting of glaciers and polar ice caps, which causes rising sea levels and threatens coastal regions.
  • Extreme Weather Events: The increase in temperature also intensifies the frequency and severity of extreme weather events such as heatwaves, droughts, floods, and hurricanes.

Key Points

  • CO₂ is the primary driver of global warming, and its levels have risen significantly due to human activity.
  • NOAA's monitoring efforts are crucial in tracking greenhouse gas concentrations globally.
  • Rising CO₂ levels are responsible for several climate-related issues, including temperature rise, ocean acidification, and severe weather events.
  • Action to reduce emissions and combat climate change is essential for the future sustainability of our planet.

Visualizing CO₂ and Climate Change

Data Analysis

In this project, we analyze data collected from various sources, including NOAA's Global Monitoring Laboratory and other reputable organizations. Our focus is on understanding the trends and impacts of greenhouse gas emissions, particularly carbon dioxide (CO₂), methane (CH₄), and nitrous oxide (N₂O). The analysis is crucial for identifying patterns, understanding the implications of these gases on climate change, and formulating effective mitigation strategies.

Data Sources

  • NOAA Global Monitoring Laboratory: Provides extensive data on greenhouse gas concentrations.
  • CO₂ Data Repository: A public repository for historical and recent CO₂ levels.
  • EPA Greenhouse Gas Reporting Program: Offers comprehensive data on greenhouse gas emissions in the U.S.

NOAA Carbon Dioxide Measurements

NOAA Carbon Dioxide (CO₂) measurements began in 1967 at the Niwot Ridge high-altitude research station in Colorado (3,523 meters above sea level). Since then, the Cooperative Carbon Cycle Greenhouse Gases (CCGG) network has expanded to measure CO₂ from over 50 locations worldwide. Continuous in-situ measurements at NOAA's Global Monitoring Laboratory (GML) Atmospheric Baseline Observatories commenced in 1973, providing essential data for understanding long-term trends in greenhouse gas concentrations. In the 1990s, additional measurements from tall towers and light aircraft in the USA were initiated to support studies on continental sources and sinks of greenhouse gases. More recently, vertical profile data from the AirCore Sampling System has been utilized since 2012 to monitor greenhouse gases at high altitudes, aiding in the validation of remote sensing measurements. These extensive measurement efforts play a crucial role in our analysis of atmospheric CO₂ and its implications for climate change.

Latitudinal Distribution of CO₂

Three-dimensional representation of the latitudinal distribution of atmospheric carbon dioxide in the marine boundary layer. Data from the Carbon Cycle cooperative air sampling network were used. The surface represents data smoothed in time and latitude.

Temporal and Spatial Variations in CO₂

Contour plot showing the temporal and spatial variations in the atmospheric increases of carbon dioxide. The cooler colors (green, blue) represent periods of lower than average growth rates, while the warmer colors (yellow, orange, red) represent periods of higher growth rates. The plot is derived from measurements of samples collected at the Carbon Cycle cooperative air sampling network sites. The variations in the growth rate of this climatically important gas are due to interannual variations in the imbalance between sources and sinks, and also to variations in atmospheric transport.
